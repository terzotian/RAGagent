Introduction to Computer Vision
What is computer vision? What is computer vision? Definition:
•Computer Vision is a field of artificial intelligence (AI) that enables computers to 
interpret, analyze , and make decisions based on visual inputs such as images, videos, 
and real -time camera feeds. •It mimics the way humans see and understand the world but with the power to 
process vast amounts of data much faster and more accurately. Example Applications:
•Facial Recognition: Identifying or verifying a person’s identity from an image or video 
frame. •Object Detection: Detecting and locating objects within an image or video stream, 
such as identifying cars, pedestrians, or animals in a scene. •Image Classification: Assigning a label to an image based on its content, like 
determining whether an image contains a cat or a dog. Important Milestones

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)
•Overview: CNNs are a class of deep neural networks specifically designed for processing 
structured grid data like images. They are composed of layers that automatically and 
adaptively learn spatial hierarchies of features from low to high -level patterns. •Components:
•Convolutional Layers: Extract features by applying filters (kernels) that slide over 
the image. •Pooling Layers: Reduce the dimensionality of the feature maps, helping to make 
the computation more efficient and capturing the most important features. •Fully Connected Layers: Flatten the matrix into a vector and feed it into a standard 
neural network to make final classifications. •Applications: Powering most modern image recognition systems, from facial recognition 
to autonomous driving.

Support Vector Machine (SVM) for Face Detection
Support Vector Machine (SVM) is a powerful supervised learning algorithm used for 
classification and regression tasks. In the context of face detection, SVM is particularly 
popular due to its efficient classification capabilities and ability to handle high -dimensional 
data effectively. How SVM Works for Face Detection

images. This involves dividing the image into smaller regions (often 
using a sliding window approach) and computing features for each 
region.
